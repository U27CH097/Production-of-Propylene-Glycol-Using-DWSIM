# Production-of-Propylene-Glycol-Using-DWSIM
This project involve Material Balance, Energy Balance, Cost Estimation and Simulation.
## Software Used : DWSIM


**"My final-year project was the simulation, design, and optimization of a propylene glycol production plant using DWSIM. The objective was to maximize product purity and plant yield while reducing raw material losses through process optimization.**

**The process starts with glycerol and hydrogen, which are fed to the plant in a molar ratio of 1:5. I selected the Peng–Robinson/Lee–Kesler thermodynamic package because the process operates at elevated pressure and contains hydrogen, making it suitable for predicting vapor–liquid equilibrium in this system.**

**The feed is first heated to 473 K before entering a conversion reactor, where glycerol reacts with hydrogen to produce propylene glycol along with small amounts of by-products and water. After the reactor, the outlet stream is cooled to 313 K and sent to a flash separator. The purpose of the flash separator is to recover unreacted hydrogen from the liquid products so that hydrogen can be recycled, reducing fresh hydrogen consumption.**

**The liquid stream is then heated to about 373 K and fed to the first distillation column operating at around 2 bar. This column removes water as the light component, while the heavier components are sent to a second vacuum distillation column for final purification. The second column separates 1,2-propylene glycol with a product purity of approximately 98%, and the overall process achieves around 95% conversion before optimization.**

**The main optimization in my project was the implementation of recycle loops. The recovered hydrogen from the flash separator was compressed and recycled back to the reactor feed, while the unreacted glycerol recovered from the vacuum distillation bottoms was also recycled to the feed stream after matching the operating temperature and pressure. This significantly reduced raw material losses and improved process economics.**

**As a result of this optimization, fresh hydrogen consumption decreased by approximately 2.5 mol/s, corresponding to about a 1.66% reduction in hydrogen utility costs. We also recovered about 1.23 mol/s (0.113 kg/s) of unreacted glycerol, reducing chemical waste to nearly zero. Because of these recycle loops, the overall plant yield increased from approximately 95% to about 99%.**

**This project helped me develop practical skills in process simulation, reactor and separation design, material and energy balances, recycle convergence, process optimization, and evaluating the effect of operating conditions on plant performance."**
